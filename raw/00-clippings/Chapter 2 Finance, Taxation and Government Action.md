To understand government actions, must understand finance & taxation, because everything costs money. But you also have to understand the government's intent, look at flows and amounts, not just read documents.

The reality is that local governments have a very large disconnect between revenues and expenditures. In recent years local govt expenditures are about 85% of total expenditures but only 50-55% of revenues. The gap has to be filled by the central government. Below we see the local % of revenue in dotted lines (~50%) and % of expenditures in black (~85%).

Tax Reform

Lots of reforms, but we'll begin with 1985. From 1985-93, as in chart above, revenues and expenditures were largely in line with each other. But it also created a lot of problems.

The 80s were characterized by the collective contracting 承包 reforms, and this extended to the tax system. Specifics differed wildly but one characteristic example is Beijing in 1988: sets 1987 as base, and adds a 4% set increase. Revenues above 4% are exclusively Beijing's, below that it is divided 50/50 between Beijing and central government. Guangdong's was even more favorable to the local government, just 13% of revenues in 1988, but Shanghai's was 65%.

But central government share of taxes grew lower and lower as % of total (dotted line) mainly because local economies grew so fast...

but the national budget as % of GDP (black line) dropped as well, because local governments were not incentivized to increase their budgets, since they renegotiated with the central government every few years and the better they did, the more they'd had to give up. Thus they tended to help companies evade taxes. A bankrupt (or poor) central government is not helpful nor desirable.

![[raw/00-clippings/images/d75fa5e17f987027e2bf3e1388380d82_MD5.png]]

1994 tax reform divided taxes into 3 types: central (such as customs), local (such as business taxes), and shared taxes (such as VAT). Two systems — national tax and local tax authorities, separated from local finance department, and provincial level and below tax authorities are vertically managed. This is to decrease local government influence over tax collection and ensure central government gets their share, but the con is also very obvious — two sets of personnel is very expensive. So in 2018, this was merged together again.

Before reform, VAT was local and 1/4 of total national revenue, after reform, it became 75% central, 25% local. Central put in some refunds to slow the decrease in funding. (Black line is VAT and dotted is business taxes.)

![[raw/00-clippings/images/25f241c2fe76d8f0c9764c0e6f8d7259_MD5.png]]

It was very difficult to convince provinces to go along with reform. Premier Zhu had to go personally. This is difficult to understand for the youth of today, but that's because the central government was a lot weaker in those days. Also, the provinces would try to play with the numbers each time a reform was put in.

Business taxes are the second largest category and accounted for 23% of revenue in 2018. Before reforms of 2002, local enterprises paid taxes to local governments, central enterprises to central. This led to a lot of protectionism in the provinces. After 2002 this was changed to 60/40 in favor of central (except for central SOEs who still pay to central).

Nowadays, central govt has about 55% share of total national budget (up from 22%), and as % of GDP it is about 20% (vs 11% before).

Land Finance

Because business tax post-reform was collected by the government where the business was established, local governments were hugely incentivized to get businesses to come.

Since a lot of taxes are collected during manufacturing, local governments emphasized this versus consumption. They also didn't care where the consumers are, just where the manufacturing was. This led to overcapacity. During 2007-2014, about half of taxes came from industries with overcapacity.

90% of taxes came from businesses, but also most other things like land use rights transfers and income from state-owned capital operations were also from businesses. So local governments spent most of time recruiting businesses vs. spending money on citizens. But central shifted strategy in 2002. However, since local expenditures suffer from economies of scale & complex information problem, it went up to 85% of total expenditures (see first graph).

Local government revenue decreased over the years —

In addition, a lot of fees, sponsorships, etc. that were collected from businesses were removed

In the reform of 2001 (as mentioned above), central government now takes 60% of the tax revenue

Thus, local governments had to come up with this "land finance"

Land Finance first steps: in China, agricultural land is collectively owned, city land is owned by the government, thus the former must be converted to the latter before you can construct commercial or residential real estate on it. Thus government land is much more valuable than farm land. But this has caused a huge disparity between urban and rural areas, surging real estate costs, etc. In proposed 2020 reforms, the first thing was to unify this gap between city and village.

In 1994 tax reform, the benefits of transferring rights of government-owned land were given to the local governments. It wasn't very much. To promote business activity, a lot of land was transferred at low costs in order to attract enterprises.

In 1998, SOEs stopped giving away housing units to employees, and the real estate market began. Also the law prevented any farm land to be used for nonfarming purposes, thus creating scarcity.

Finally we end up with the below. It was fine in 1999-2000, but quickly, revenue from transferring government land as % of local government budget increased significantly.

![[raw/00-clippings/images/228a93ed05541b65c9c22ab700390e91_MD5.png]]

It was already at 55% in 2003 but increased to 68% in 2010 after the financial crisis. Although the relative % has declined a little bit, the absolute value is still very high. In 2018 it was 6.2Trn RMB, 2.3x that of 2010

Land finance doesn't just include revenues from transferring rights, but also usage and development taxes. And most of the revenue comes from the value of the land and not its area.

In 2018, just from land VAT, usage taxes, arable land occupation tax and deed taxes, this was about 1.5Trn RMB & 100% went to local government making up 15% of local budget

Real estate developers also pay taxes but only 50% of VAT and 40% of income tax goes to local governments, making up 9% of local budget

If we add these to the 65% from transfer rights, then it really makes up 89% of local government budgets

But such revenues come with expenses. In 2018 the 6.2Trn RMB came with 6.8Trn RMB expenses.

Unlike pre-2001 when it was all about industrialization, it has been industrialization + urbanization in the last 20 years. 1/2 of area of land transferred has been for industry, but the price is very low — +85% in 18 years (2000-2018). Over same time period, commercial land increased 4.6x, residential 7.4x. See below for price per sqm for commercial (dotted), residential (dash), industrial (black line).

![[raw/00-clippings/images/3eee571a2c50ea4b88ec5a60ac432a68_MD5.png]]

Thus really it is everything else, especially residential, subsidizing industrial development.

But why can't they raise money for industrial land? Because there's a lot of choice. But far less choice for residents.

Debt is a problem, to be discussed in a later chapter, but so is inefficient allocation. Local governments try to balance land use but it is inefficiently allocated from a national perspective. We see still farmland in Shanghai, and wasted industrial parks in the West. Thus in 2020, an opinion was issued for a unified land use market.

Vertical Imbalance and Horizontal Imbalance

After tax reform, central government took most of the revenues but expenditures stayed with the local government, with the gap needing to be filled by payments transferred from central.

Also, provincial and city / village have a split as well, so "money goes upwards, tasks flow downward"

Since land values differed so much, poorer regions didn't have enough money and so made up all sorts of fees, leading to lots of conflict in the 90s and early 00s. In 2006, agricultural taxes were thoroughly abolished, a landmark event

Another thing was rural public services was now borne by both central and local governments. This is for education, healthcare, etc.

Decrease of staff. (Although initial dip in 2005 was reversed by 2008, and no disclosures after that.)

More centralization. By 2012, 86% of village finances were managed by counties. To prevent cities from abusing their power over counties, counties were given more rights to be more on par with cities

In a way, this flattened the 5-level structure to 3 levels: central-province-city/village. This worked well for a province like Zhejiang but didn't work for most other provinces, leading to more diffuse development and slower growth of GDP per capita

Overall central government transfers to the central & west regions increased 93x from 1995 to 2018, from 1% to 7% of GDP. 80% of the transfers were to the central & west regions. Even though disparities are high, without those transfers, they would be even higher. 2 types of transfers, discretionary & non discretionary (40%, usually by project). The latter actually often increases inequality because only the best developed areas can get the best projects.
